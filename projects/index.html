Our lab asks how cells sense and respond to changes in their environment. We study the cellular stress response, the roles of molecular chaperones as regulators of biomolecular condensation, and how cells sense temperature. Biomolecular condensation—the assembly of cellular components into concentrated, spatially organized bodies—has emerged as a unifying mechanism across all of these areas.
Rethinking the cellular stress response
image coming soon
For decades, the dominant view has been that stress damages cells and that the stress response exists to repair that damage. Our work has overturned this view, showing that many stress-triggered changes—including massive protein aggregation—are adaptive, evolved responses rather than signs of damage. We have shown that condensation of proteins and mRNAs during stress is tuned by evolution, regulated by conserved molecular machinery, and linked to cellular fitness.

Molecular chaperones as condensation regulators
image coming soon
Molecular chaperones are best known for helping proteins fold. We have discovered that chaperones also play a major role in regulating biomolecular condensation. Our studies reveal that chaperones can disperse condensates, reshape their composition, and control their formation through a regulated sequence of activation steps.
Key papers:
  • Bard & Drummond, Frontiers in Biophysics 2024 — Chaperone regulation of biomolecular condensates
  • Chen et al., PNAS 2024 — HDX-MS finds that partial unfolding with sequential domain activation controls condensation of a cellular stress marker
  • Ali et al., Nat. Cell Biol. 2023 — Adaptive preservation of orphan ribosomal proteins in chaperone-stirred condensates
  • Yoo et al., Mol. Cell 2022 — Chaperones directly and efficiently disperse stress-triggered biomolecular condensates
How cells sense temperature
image coming soon
Temperature affects virtually every biological process, yet how cells actually sense temperature remains poorly understood. We have found that biomolecular condensation can act as a molecular thermometer: phase-separation temperatures of key proteins are tuned to organism-specific thermal thresholds. This work connects physical chemistry to cellular decision-making and even to circadian rhythms.
Key papers:
  • Keyport Kik et al., Nat. Commun. 2024 — An adaptive biomolecular condensation response is conserved across environmentally divergent species
  • Pattanayak et al., Cell Reports 2020 — Daily cycles of reversible protein condensation in cyanobacteria
  • Triandafillou et al., eLife 2020 — Transient intracellular acidification regulates the core transcriptional heat shock response
  • Yoo et al., JBC 2019 — Cellular sensing by phase separation: Using the process, not just the products
  • Riback, Katanski et al., Cell 2017 — Stress-triggered phase separation is an adaptive, evolutionarily tuned response
